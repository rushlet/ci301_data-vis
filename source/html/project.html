A Visual Analysis of UK Number 1s

Becky Rush

This data visualisation project highlights the most successful songs in UK chart history.

It considers how certain attributes of songs have changed over time and showcases some of the most surprising songs to have made music history.

The UK Music Charts began on 14th November 1952

In the 3398 weeks between then and the 1st of January 2018, 1325 different songs have reached number 1

Out of these, only 2 are held by Grammy award-winning singer Adele.
The same number as children's cartoon character Bob the Builder.

Similarly, music legends Jimi Hendrix and Prince both only hold one number 1 each - the same number as the Teletubbies.

Let's take a closer look at the artists who have topped the charts

This graph shows the distribution of artists who have lasted the longest at number 1. The size of each bubble represents the number of tracks that have contributed to the length of time.

Elvis is the King of the Charts, with 18 UK number 1s. The Beatles just miss out, with 17.

Unsurprisingly, Elvis and The Beatles have also spent the longest time at number 1, with 79 and 69 weeks respectively.

Other successful artists, in terms of most number 1s, include Cliff Richard (14), Westlife (14) and Madonna (13).

Frankie Laine has held the number 1 position for 32 weeks - the 5th longest serving artist. He did this with only 4 songs reaching number 1.

This could be because they were in 1953, when the charts were just beginning so there may have been less competition.

However, Wet Wet Wet have also held the position for a substantial amount of time (23 weeks) with only 3 tracks, thanks to the success of ‘Love is all Around’ in 1994.

Justin Bieber has spent the same amount of time at the top of the charts as Madonna and Take That, despite having half the number of songs reach number 1. They have each held the position for 29 weeks, with 6, 13 and 12 songs respectively.

Let's look a little deeper into the audio features of number 1 songs.

Spotify attaches a variety of attributes to each of the songs in their library. Described as ‘audio features’, these include danceability, valence (‘feel goodness’), energy, acousticness and loudness.

The attributes are generated by EchoNest, who employ a range of techniques to quantify them. For example, danceability is calculated by considering overall tempo and beat strength, whereas valence used the opinion of a musical expert which was then expanded upon using machine learning.

The following analysis is based on the values provided by Spotify for the songs which have reached number 1. 5 songs have been ommited as they are not available on Spotify.

Danceability

The most danceable songs that have reached number 1 are Ice Ice Baby by Vanilla Ice and Give It To Me by Timberland, Nelly Furtado and Justin Timberlake.

Arguabley, Justin Timberlake is the most danceable artist, holding both the joint first most danceable number one and the second most danceable (SexyBack).

Surprisingly, Eminem holds 3 of the top 10 - The Real Slim Shady, Just Lose It and Smack That (collaboration with Akon).

The least danceable song that has reached number 1 is Westlife’s You Raise Me Up.

Valence

The most ‘feel good’ song is Madness’ House of Fun, with a valence score of 98.5%. This is closely followed by Bucks Fizz ‘Making your mind up’.

The song with the lowest valence score is Amazing Grace, as performed by the Pipes drums dragon band… followed by Celine Dion’s My Heart Will Go On.

Acousticness

The most acoustic song to reach number 1 is Whispering Grass by Windsor Davies & Don Estelle. I Believe by Frankie Laine and Mad World by Michael Andrews and Gary Jules follow closely behind.

The least acoustic ones are Manic Street Preachers' The Masses Against The Classes and The Hindu Times by Oasis.

Energy

The highest energy songs to reach the top spot are No Limit by 2 Unlimited with a huge 99.9% energy. Close behind with 99.7% is Mr Vain by Culture Beat and Ooh Aah... Just A Little Bit by Gina G with 99.6%.

The lowest scoring song in terms of energy is Albatross by Fleetwood Mac (3.82%).

Now we've seen the examples of the extremes for some of the audio attributes, let's look at how they might have changed over time

This graph shows the mean average of a few of the audio features by year, allowing us to see see which years had the most changes musically.

Acousticness

We can see that generally, acousticness has been declining.

It reached its lowest point (on average) in 2009, with songs such as La Roux’s Bulletproof and Lady GaGa’s Bad Romance.

It then spiked in 2015 thanks to more acoustic tracks taking the scene, thanks to artists such as Sam Smith and Justin Bieber.

Danceability

Danceability has generally been increasing over the years.

It saw its peak in 1983. This year saw some pretty danceable classics, including Michael Jackson's Billie Jean, Give It Up by KC & The Sunshine Band and Red Red Wine By Ub40.

Danceability plummeted in 1967, the year when songs including Nancy and Frank Sinatra's Something' Stupid and The Beatles' All You Need Is Love made it big.

Valence

There is less of an obvious trend for valence than other audio features, but it seems to have generally declined since the 60s.

It reached its highest in 1963, with songs such as From Me To You, by The Beatles.

Most number 1s had high valence scores this year, even the lowest scoring ones were pretty feel good - such as Cliff Richard’s Summer Holiday (the 3rd lowest in 1963).

On average, the least feel good year was 1954, featuring songs such as Secret Love by Doris Day.

The least feel good year since 1954 is 1996.
Curiously, both of these were the year before the current Conservative prime minister was replaced.
(Churchill by Conservative candidate Eden in 1955 and Major by Labour's Tony Blair)

Furthermore, a few major events happened in 1996, including the Dunblance Massacre, IRA bombing in Manchester, Charles and Diana divorcing and Take That splitting up.

Could it be that these events had an impact on the happiness of music produced that year?
